# 02 - Conversations: Munger's Q&A Patterns and Impromptu Thinking

*Research compiled from Wesco 1999-2011 and DJCO 2014-2020 meeting notes, speech Q&A sections, and interview transcripts.*

---

## 1. Q&A Patterns

### Pattern: "I Have Nothing to Add" — The Classic Deflect

Munger's most famous Q&A response became so iconic that Buffett once used a cardboard cutout of Munger playing a recording of "I have nothing to add" at a special shareholders meeting. This is his primary technique for questions outside his competence, questions he's answered repeatedly, or questions where he's simply unwilling to engage.

**Example 1**
- **Source**: 2001-2006-Mungerisms.md (compilation)
- **Context**: At Berkshire annual meetings, Buffett typically answers at length, then turns to Munger: "Charlie, what do you think?"
- **Munger's response**: "I have nothing to add."
- **Analysis**: These four words became his trademark. They serve multiple functions simultaneously: refusing to waste time repeating Buffett, signaling consensus, deflecting questions outside his competency, and preserving the mystique of silence. The humor arises from the predictability — audiences laugh because they expect it.

**Example 2**
- **Source**: 2006 Wesco meeting notes
- **Question**: Munger criticized accountants for not reconciling derivative trades where both sides booked profits on the same trade. He then turned to his accountant on stage: "I'll give our accountant the opportunity if he wishes to participate in this discussion and explain why the accountants are doing what they're doing."
- **Munger's response**: (After a pause) "He says he has nothing to add." (Laughter)
- **Analysis**: Munger brilliantly uses his own catchphrase as a weapon — imposing it on someone else to make a point about professional cowardice. The humor masks a devastating critique.

**Example 3**
- **Source**: 2018 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: "Could you comment on whether you ever considered investments in Hershey's or Tiffany's?"
- **Munger's answer**: "Well I'd be delighted to own either Hershey's or Tiffany's at the right price, wouldn't you? It's just a question of price. Of course they're great companies. But that's not enough, you have to have great companies available at a price you're willing to pay. Hershey's is a private company. Nobody's offering me Hershey's. I can buy the candy, but I can't buy the company."
- **Analysis**: Here Munger gives a substantive answer to what could have been deflected. He engages when the question touches on core principles (price vs. value). He only deflects with "nothing to add" when the question is unproductive or outside his wheelhouse.

### Pattern: Circle of Competence Enforcement

Munger is brutally disciplined about staying within his circle — and equally blunt about admitting when he's outside it.

**Example 1**
- **Source**: 2018 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: "Your thoughts on the valuation of software companies like Apple, Facebook, Google, Amazon, Alibaba. Are they over-valued, potentially under-valued, too early to tell?"
- **Munger's answer**: "Well my answer is I don't know. (laughter) Next question. (laughter)"
- **Analysis**: Instant shutdown. No pretense, no hedging. Munger wastes zero words. This is the purest form of circle-of-competence enforcement: when he genuinely doesn't know, he says so and moves on without embarrassment. The speed of the deflection communicates that pretending to know would be the real sin.

**Example 2**
- **Source**: 2015 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: "What do you think of new Internet platforms for starting companies such as Kickstarter?"
- **Munger's answer**: "I don't know anything about new Internet platforms. (laughter)"
- **Analysis**: The laugh comes because this is such a perfect, clean Munger shutdown. No apology, no awkwardness — just a statement of fact and a willingness to be done.

**Example 3**
- **Source**: 2016 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: "Do you have a specific approach to spending quality time with your family?"
- **Munger's answer**: "Well, I don't think I want to promote myself as some wonderful example of family life. I did the best I could…"
- **Analysis**: Unusual vulnerability. Munger deflects not because he lacks competence but because he's honest about limitations in his personal life.

**Example 4**
- **Source**: 2016 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: "What's your view on the Unicorn companies like Airbnb, Uber, etc. Do you think those companies have such high valuations can ever go public?"
- **Munger's answer**: "Well, my attitude is that I have a circle of competence. And that does not include correctly predicting which new companies in Silicon Valley, or dependent on Silicon Valley, are going to succeed. So I tend to avoid the subject entirely."
- **Analysis**: Textbook circle-of-competence response. He names the concept explicitly, explains why it's outside, and closes the door firmly.

### Pattern: The "Too Hard" Pile

Munger frequently invokes his mental filing system — problems go into three baskets: "In," "Out," and "Too Hard."

**Example 1**
- **Source**: 2002 Wesco meeting notes
- **Context**: Describing his investment decision process
- **Munger's words**: "There are a lot of things we pass on. We have three baskets: in, out and too tough. A lot of stuff goes into the 'too tough' basket. We can't do that if it's a problem at a Berkshire subsidiary company, but if we don't own it, we just pass."

**Example 2**
- **Source**: 2019 DJCO meeting notes
- **Munger's words**: "Part of our secret is that we don't attempt to know a lot of things. I have a pile on my desk that solves most of my problems, it's called the 'too hard' pile. And I just keep shifting things to the 'too hard' pile. And every once in a while an easy decision comes along and I make it. That's my system. Everything was the 'too hard' pile, except for a few easy decisions which I make, promptly."

**Example 3**
- **Source**: 2008 Wesco meeting notes
- **Question**: (About whether one needs a checklist for investing)
- **Munger's answer**: "I don't have a simple checklist. You have to work at it a long long time. I still do dumb things after years of hard work. The more big ideas you have the easier. We exclude a whole lot of things because they are in the too tough pile. If you exclude, you do better."

**Analysis across examples**: The "too hard" pile is Munger's most important mental tool. It's not laziness — it's a disciplined recognition of cognitive limits. He treats the admission "this is too hard for me" as a sign of intelligence, not weakness. This pattern appears consistently from 2002 through 2019, showing it's a lifelong habit, not a later-life convenience.

### Pattern: When Challenged or Pushed Back

Munger responds to challenges with withering directness, often using humor to disarm while doubling down.

**Example 1**
- **Source**: 2015 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: "I'd like to get your thoughts on American Express. Do you think its moat has narrowed recently?"
- **Context**: Munger explained Amex lost Costco contract, demonstrating intense competition.
- **Munger's answer**: "It just shows how tough a position that looks impregnable can be in modern capitalism. It's what makes everything difficult. ... We paddled hard here too, and what good did it do us in Daily Journal's print business? We paddled like crazy, didn't we Gerry?" (Gerry: "We tried. It was hard.") "Yeah, what happened is you just keep receding and receding. Welcome to adult life."
- **Analysis**: Rather than getting defensive, Munger universalizes the challenge — "welcome to adult life" reframes the setback as normal rather than exceptional. He includes his own failures, neutralizing any implied criticism.

**Example 2**
- **Source**: 2019 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: "Why is Warren so much richer than you?"
- **Munger's answer**: "Well. He got an earlier start. He's probably a little smarter. He works harder. There are not a lot of reasons. Why was Albert Einstein poorer than I was? (laughter)"
- **Analysis**: Confronted with what could be an awkward or competitive question, Munger disarms completely with honesty and self-deprecation. The Einstein comparison reframes the entire premise — wealth and worth are different scales.

**Example 3**
- **Source**: 2006 Wesco meeting notes
- **Question**: (A shareholder asked a complex multi-part question about activist hedge funds, pointing out an apparent inconsistency in Munger/Buffett's position.)
- **Munger's answer**: "The reason I said it was a mixed bag is because I regard it as a mixed bag. ... Tweedy Browne was revolted by the behavior and so they got on a real moral crusade. And they were right, and I applaud that. But a lot of other people just want to raise hell and they don't care if they're raising hell with admirable people or knaves — they're just trying to make money."
- **Analysis**: When challenged on a perceived inconsistency, Munger gives ground on the specific case (praising Tweedy Browne) while reinforcing his broader point. He makes distinctions rather than retreating.

### Pattern: Asked About Macroeconomic Predictions

Munger has a standard, almost ritualized refusal to engage with macro predictions — often delivered with humor.

**Example 1**
- **Source**: 2001 Wesco meeting notes
- **Munger's words**: "Neither Warren nor I has ever made a dime in this area." (On predicting interest rates)

**Example 2**
- **Source**: 2004 Wesco meeting notes
- **Munger's words**: "Gigantic macroeconomic predictions are something I've never made any money on, and neither has Warren."

**Example 3**
- **Source**: 2006 Wesco meeting notes
- **Question**: "Should one be worried about having cash in a brokerage account?"
- **Munger's answer**: "I think a cash account at the really strong brokerages is safe enough. You have other better things to worry about."

**Example 4**
- **Source**: 2007 Wesco meeting notes
- **Question**: "Why hasn't the declining dollar led to increased inflation?"
- **Munger's answer**: "It's a good question. A lot of economics professors would say it couldn't happen, but it did. It's very important. But how it matters — if I were able to do that, I wouldn't be qualified to sit here. The answer is, I don't know."

**Example 5**
- **Source**: 2015 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: About persistent negative interest rates.
- **Munger's answer**: "I was flabbergasted when they went low; when they went negative in Europe — I'm really flabbergasted. How many in this room would have predicted negative interest rates in Europe? Raise your hands. [No hands go up] That's exactly the way I feel. How can I be an expert in something I never even thought about that seems so unlikely. It's new territory… If you find it puzzling, your brain is working correctly."

**Analysis**: This is one of Munger's most consistent patterns. He never pretends to predict macro outcomes. His standard move is to: (1) admit ignorance, (2) note that neither he nor Buffett ever profited from macro bets, (3) occasionally use the Keynes quote or Herb Stein's "if something cannot go on forever, it will stop," and (4) redirect to micro factors they can actually analyze. The phrase "neither Warren nor I has ever made a dime" appears verbatim multiple times across decades.

### Pattern: Reframing the Question

Munger frequently declines to answer the question as asked and instead answers a better question he wishes had been asked.

**Example 1**
- **Source**: 2007 Wesco meeting notes
- **Question**: "Can you give an example of giving up a closely held idea?"
- **Munger's answer**: "I'm answering the question I'm capable of answering, instead of the one you asked. I invert: I try to figure out what I don't like and try to avoid it. It's worked wonders for me."

**Example 2**
- **Source**: 2017 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: About a foundation invested in three stocks.
- **Munger's initial response**: "Well, let's take the foundation…I'll change your question around (in the way that I want to answer it). (laughter) Am I comfortable with a non-diversified portfolio? Of course…if you take the Munger's, I care about the Munger's. The Munger's have three stocks."

**Example 3**
- **Source**: 2008 Wesco meeting notes
- **Munger's opening format**: "I will talk before I take your questions. I will address two topics, general investment climate and learnings from Berkshire Hathaway. We normally avoid discussing the general investment climate like the plague."
- **Analysis**: Munger pre-emptively sets the agenda. He calls this "Socratic solitaire" — asking and answering his own questions before the audience gets a turn.

## 2. Impromptu Analogies

Munger deploys analogies spontaneously in Q&A — they are his primary thinking tool.

### Business Analogies

**1. The Fishing Tackle Salesman**
- **Source**: 1994 USC Business School Speech (also referenced in meeting notes)
- **Full passage**: "I think the reason why we got into such idiocy in investment management is best illustrated by a story that I tell about the guy who sold fishing tackle. I asked him, 'My God, they're purple and green. Do fish really take these lures?' And he said, 'Mister, I don't sell to fish.'"
- **Analysis**: Munger's most-used analogy about misaligned incentives. He applies it to investment managers who sell products good for themselves, not their clients. The analogy's power is in its simplicity — anyone can grasp it instantly.

**2. The Pari-Mutuel Betting System**
- **Source**: 1994 USC Speech (repeated throughout meeting notes)
- **Full passage**: "The model I like — to sort of simplify the notion of what goes on in a market for common stocks — is the pari-mutuel system at the racetrack. If you stop to think about it, a pari-mutuel system is a market. Everybody goes there and bets and the odds change based on what's bet. That's what happens in the stock market."
- **Analysis**: This is Munger's master analogy for understanding markets. The key insight: it's possible to beat the system, but only for those who bet rarely and heavily when they have an edge. Used to explain why concentrated investing works.

**3. Shooting Fish in a Barrel (with variations)**
- **Source**: Multiple DJCO meetings
- **Full passage** (2015): "My idea of shooting fish in a barrel, by the way, is to drain the barrel first. (laughter) It's really easy, then you shoot, preferably with a big shotgun."
- **Full passage** (2017): "In the old days, I frequently talked to Warren about the old days, and for years and years and years, what we did was shoot fish in a barrel. But it was so easy that we didn't want to shoot at the fish while they were moving. So we waited until they slowed down and then we shot at them with shotgun. It was just that easy. And it has gotten harder and harder and harder."
- **Analysis**: This analogy evolves over time — a meta-example of Munger updating his own metaphors as conditions change. The "drain the barrel first" elaboration (2015) shows his trademark one-upmanship even on his own analogies.

**4. The Builder's Scaffolding / Parachute Packing**
- **Source**: 2008 Wesco meeting notes
- **Full passage**: "In Rome, the builder and designer stood under the bridge when the scaffolding was removed. In parachutes, you pack your own chute. Capitalism works that way too. At a restaurant, owner is bearing the consequences."
- **Analysis**: The liability/responsibility alignment analogy. Munger returns to this theme constantly — those who make decisions must bear the consequences.

**5. Rats in a Granary**
- **Source**: 2016 & 2017 DJCO meeting notes
- **Full passage**: "I think that high frequency traders of the world, many of whom are personally admirable and honorable people, I think they have all made contributions to the American economy like a bunch of rats do in a granary. (laughter) They're just sucking some of the resources out for themselves while contributing nothing to the civilization."
- **Full passage** (2017): "I always say that those people have all the social utility of a bunch of rats in a granary. It's not that great a way to make money. I would say if you make your money that way that you should be very charitable with it because you've got a lot to atone for."
- **Analysis**: This savage analogy recurs. Munger applies it to high-frequency traders, algorithm traders, and derivative operators — anyone who extracts value without creating it. Note the escalation: first he uses it for HFT, then broadens it to all computer algorithm traders.

**6. Raisins and Turds**
- **Source**: 2000 Wesco meeting notes
- **Full passage**: "Because it's mixed with legitimate activities like venture capital, it looks respectable. But we're mixing respectable activity with disrespectable activity — hence my comment at the Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting about if you mix raisins with turds, you've still got turds."
- **Analysis**: Vintage Munger — crude, memorable, and devastating. Targets earnings manipulation and accounting fraud.

**7. Casino in Drag**
- **Source**: 2015 DJCO meeting notes
- **Full passage**: "And by the way, a lot of our major capitalistic institutions that parade as really respectable, they're just casinos in drag. What do you think a derivative trading desk is? It's a casino in drag."
- **Analysis**: This analogy captures Munger's thesis that much of modern finance is gambling dressed in respectable clothing. The image is deliberately provocative.

**8. One-Legged Man in an Ass-Kicking Contest**
- **Source**: 1994 USC Speech, repeated in 2017 DJCO
- **Full passage**: "If you don't get this elementary, but mildly unnatural, mathematics of elementary probability into your repertoire, then you go through a long life like a one-legged man in an ass-kicking contest. You're giving a huge advantage to everybody else."
- **Analysis**: Applied to those who lack basic mental models. The coarseness makes it unforgettable.

**9. The Undertaker in a Plague Year / Town with the Plague**
- **Source**: 2014 & 2016 DJCO meeting notes
- **2014**: "They were the type that come along very rarely and they happened to coincide with a time when we were the only undertaker in a town with the plague."
- **2016**: "We had more than 80% of the foreclosure notice business, and it was like being an undertaker in a plague year."
- **Analysis**: Dark humor to describe windfall profits from the foreclosure crisis. The consistency of the analogy across years is notable.

**10. The Man with a Hammer**
- **Source**: 1994 USC Speech, repeated endlessly
- **Full passage**: "It's like the old saying, 'To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail.'"
- **Analysis**: Possibly Munger's single most-quoted analogy. It encapsulates his entire philosophy about multidisciplinary thinking.

**11. The Northern Pike Model**
- **Source**: 2010 Wesco meeting notes
- **Full passage**: "Some businesses are like Northern Pike and come into the business world of stupid trout and gallop through the world like Genghis Kahn. Costco and Wal-Mart are examples of these types of companies."
- **Analysis**: A rare ecological analogy. Munger views business competition through a biological/ecological lens — predators and prey, ecosystems and niches.

**12. Hydrogen and Oxygen / Lighting a Match**
- **Source**: 2005 Wesco meeting notes
- **Full passage**: "Any time you let people on sales commission set the credit standards for people using margin [e.g., debt to buy the home], you create a disaster. It's like mixing oxygen and hydrogen and lighting a match."
- **Analysis**: Uses chemical imagery to describe systemic risk. The inevitability of the explosion is the point — the conditions make disaster certain.

### Personal/Life Analogies

**13. The Man Who Jumped Off the Skyscraper**
- **Source**: 2007 Wesco meeting notes
- **Full passage**: "I'm getting more experienced at aging. I'm like the man who jumped off the skyscraper and at the 5th floor on the way down says, 'So far this is not a bad ride.' (Laughter) I'm getting better with aging. I'm not going to complain about age because if I didn't have it, I'd be dead."
- **Analysis**: Classic Munger gallows humor about mortality. He uses this joke to both acknowledge and transcend the reality of aging.

**14. The Mozart Anecdote**
- **Source**: 2004 Wesco, 2015 DJCO, 2017 DJCO, 2019 DJCO
- **Full passage** (2017): "A young man comes to see Mozart, and says, 'I want to compose symphonies.' And Mozart says, 'You're too young to compose symphonies.' He's 20 years old and the man says, 'But you were composing symphonies when you were 10 years old.' And Mozart says, 'Yeah but I wasn't running around asking other people how to do it.'"
- **Analysis**: This story appears in at least four different meeting years. Munger uses it when asked for career/investing advice — the point being that asking others for the secret formula is itself evidence you don't have what it takes. It's both harsh and profound.

**15. Ben Franklin's Marriage Advice**
- **Source**: 2008 Wesco, 2017 Michigan talk, 2015 DJCO
- **Full passage**: "Ben Franklin gave the best advice, keep your eyes wide open before marriage and half shut thereafter."
- **Analysis**: Munger uses Franklin constantly as an authority figure. The marriage advice becomes a metaphor for how to approach life's unchangeable difficulties.

**16. The Plumber's Quote / Firing the Top Producer**
- **Source**: 2002 Wesco meeting notes
- **Full passage**: "It reminds me of a guy running a company who fired his top producer. The guy asked him, 'Why are you firing me? I'm your top producer.' To which he responded, 'You make me nervous. I'm a rich old man. Why should I be nervous?'"
- **Analysis**: Used to explain why Berkshire exited Freddie Mac — not because of fraud, but because the leverage made them nervous. The analogy makes the abstract principle concrete.

**17. The Cost-Plus Mule Contractor**
- **Source**: 2020 DJCO meeting notes
- **Full passage**: "The owner of the mules said, 'I can't do that.' The big builder asked: 'Why not?' and the contractor said: 'I get business all these years because I'm so efficient. But when I take a cost-plus contract even my mules seem to know it and they all go to hell.' And that's the Daily Journal's policy."
- **Analysis**: Brilliant analogy about misaligned incentives. Munger uses it to explain why DJCO refuses to bill by the hour — they only get paid when the system works. The mules "knowing" is the proxy for human nature responding to incentives.

## 3. Position Changes — Views That Evolved

### Railroads: From "Terrible Business" to Core Investment

- **Source**: 2007 Wesco meeting notes
- **Early view**: "Warren and I have hated railroads our entire life. They're capital-intensive, heavily unionized, with some make-work rules, heavily regulated, and long competed with a comparative disadvantage vs. the trucking industry... Railroads have long been a terrible business and have been lousy for investors."
- **Change**: "We did finally change our minds and invested. We threw out our paradigms, but did it too late. We should have done it two years ago, but we were too stupid to do it at the most ideal time. There's a German saying: Man is too soon old and too late smart. We were too late smart."
- **Analysis**: This is Munger's most-cited example of changing his mind. He publicly mocks his own slowness — "Bill Gates figured this out years before us." The lesson: even the best investors can be trapped by outdated mental models. It took ~75 years for railroads to become good businesses again, and Munger/Buffett took 50 years to notice.

### Airlines: From Joke to Investment

- **Source**: 2017 DJCO meeting notes
- **Evolution**: "Think of the hooting we've done over the years about high tech, 'we just don't understand it', 'it's not in our central competency', 'the worst business in the world is airlines'. And what do we appear in the press with? Apple and a bunch of airlines. I don't think we've went crazy. I think the answer is, we're adapting reasonably to a business that's gotten very much more difficult."
- **Analysis**: The airline pivot is even more dramatic than railroads. Munger had spent decades making jokes about airlines destroying capital. The justification is notable: the world changed (consolidation to 4 major carriers), and their opportunity set shrank (less easy money available). He's honest that these aren't cinches — just bets where the odds are slightly favorable.

### From Cigar-Butt Investing to Great Businesses at Fair Prices

- **Source**: 2002 Wesco meeting notes
- **Evolution**: "Warren trained under this system and made money, so he was slower to come to the idea I learned that the best way to make money is to buy great businesses that earn high returns on capital over long periods of time."
- **Source**: 2011 Munger's Thoughts (speech)
- **Further**: "When we bought See's Candies, we didn't know the power of a good brand. Over time we just discovered that we could raise prices 10% a year and no one cared. Learning that changed Berkshire. It was really important."
- **Analysis**: The most consequential evolution in Munger's investing career. He started as a Graham-style bargain hunter and evolved into a quality-focused investor. See's Candies was the epiphany. This evolution is so well-documented that it's become a core part of the Munger origin story — yet he's clear it happened gradually, through experience, not through any single insight.

### Technology: From Complete Avoidance to Selective Investment

- **Source**: 2010 Wesco meeting notes
- **Evolution**: "Up until recently, Charlie and Warren have shunned high tech. ... Why are they doing this? Well, their past behavior was due to inadequacies. The change happened because they developed other models." (Then describes BYD and Iscar.)
- **Source**: 2017 DJCO meeting notes
- **Further evolution**: (On buying Apple) "We bought the Exxon position… You know why Warren bought Exxon? As a cash substitute! You would never have done that in the old days. ... I don't think we have a cinch in either of those positions. I think we have the odds a little bit in our favor. And if that's the best advantage we can get, we'll just have to live on the advantage we can get."
- **Analysis**: Munger frames the tech evolution as adaptation to a harder environment, not as becoming smarter. Apple was bought as better-than-cash; airlines were bought because consolidation improved the industry structure. He's explicit: these are not cinches, just bets with slightly favorable odds — a downgrade from their historical standards.

### Fixed Income Arbitrage: From Scorn to Moderation

- **Source**: 2006 Wesco meeting notes
- **Evolution**: "I had more scorn for fixed income arbitrage in the past than I do now. I was also more hopeful about troubles in the Middle East than I am now. So I've destroyed some of my optimism in favor of more realism."
- **Analysis**: Rare self-correction on a specific investment strategy. Munger explicitly uses this as an example of "destroying your best-loved ideas" — one of his core principles.

### The Adjusting View on Newspapers

- **Source**: 2000 Wesco meeting notes (optimistic): The newspaper business was "way less certain to be a wonderful business" but still viable.
- **Source**: 2006 Wesco meeting notes: "I think their future is way worse than their past."
- **Source**: 2020 DJCO meeting notes (fatalistic): "Berkshire Hathaway owns — what — about a hundred of them, and truth of the matter is they're all going to die and there's nothing that can be done with good management to save them."
- **Analysis**: A 20-year arc from cautious optimism to complete fatalism. The speed of technological destruction surprised even Munger, who had warned about it early.

## 4. Refused Questions — Topics Munger Declines

### Category A: "What are you buying/selling?"

- **Source**: 2003 Wesco meeting notes
- **Question**: About Berkshire's investment in Mueller Industries.
- **Munger's response**: "We ordinarily don't talk about the reasons for making particular investments. We don't want people following us into particular stocks. It's sort of like asking, 'What are you buying tomorrow?' We tend not to answer those questions."
- **Source**: 2008 Wesco meeting notes
- **Munger's opening rule**: "Do not ask what we are buying or selling. Any other question is fair game, but we don't agree to answer them."
- **Analysis**: This is Munger's most consistently enforced boundary. It's stated as a ground rule before Q&A begins in multiple years. The refusal is practical — they don't want front-running — not philosophical.

### Category B: Macroeconomic and Market Predictions

- **Source**: 2001 Wesco meeting notes: "Neither Warren nor I has ever made a dime in this area." (interest rate predictions)
- **Source**: 2004 Wesco meeting notes: "Gigantic macroeconomic predictions are something I've never made any money on, and neither has Warren."
- **Source**: 2003 Wesco meeting notes: "Ordinarily, [my advice is to] buy housing when you need it, and don't try to time the market."

### Category C: Technologies He Doesn't Understand

- **Source**: 2015 DJCO meeting notes: "I don't know anything about new Internet platforms." (Kickstarter)
- **Source**: 2018 DJCO meeting notes: "I'm not studying artificial intelligence because I wouldn't be able to learn much about it." (AI)
- **Source**: 2015 DJCO meeting notes: "I do not understand how they get so much information through space at the same time. ... It's very complicated. I don't understand it. I understand peanut brittle."

### Category D: Personal Pain and Worst Experiences

- **Source**: 2007 Wesco meeting notes
- **Question**: "Can you tell us about your worst experience and how you dealt with it?"
- **Munger's response**: "I'm not going to answer that."
- **Analysis**: Flat refusal. No humor, no deflection — just a boundary. This is unusual in Munger's Q&A style and signals a genuine unwillingness to go there.

### Category E: "I Don't Want to Talk About It"

- **Source**: 2006 Wesco meeting notes
- **Question**: "Why didn't you invest in K-Mart or Tyco?"
- **Munger's response**: "I didn't think about K-Mart. As for Tyco, I thought about and I passed, but I don't want to tell you the reason."
- **Analysis**: Asymmetrically opaque. Munger will share successes and some failures but draws lines around certain names. The deliberate mystery is itself a message.

### Category F: "I Can't Help You" / Career Advice

- **Source**: 2019 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: "Which money managers would you recommend besides you and Warren?"
- **Munger's response**: "Well I just said I've only hired one myself in a lifetime, I don't think that makes me an expert."
- **Source**: 2015 DJCO meeting notes
- **Question**: About finding a misunderstood moat.
- **Munger's response**: "You're going to ask a 91-year-old man how to do it? Reminds me of one of my favorite stories. A young man comes to Mozart ..." [tells the Mozart anecdote]

## 5. Memorable One-Liners

These are Munger's most distinctive communication feature — compressed wisdom delivered with a sharp edge.

### On Investing and Business

1. **"If you mix raisins with turds, you've still got turds."** (2000 Wesco) — On earnings manipulation mixed with legitimate business.

2. **"It's not the bad ideas that do you in. It's the good ideas that get you."** (1999 Wesco, quoting Ben Graham) — On overdoing good investment themes.

3. **"You make your money by the waiting."** (2016 DJCO, quoting an unnamed investor) — On patience as the investor's primary tool.

4. **"We have three baskets: in, out and too tough."** (2002 Wesco) — The "too hard" pile philosophy compressed into one sentence.

5. **"If it's trite, it's right."** (2001, 2003 Wesco; 2019 DJCO) — His defense of simple, ancient wisdom against intellectual novelty-seeking.

6. **"We don't give a damn about lumpy results. Everyone else is trying to please Wall Street. This is not a small advantage."** (2000 Wesco) — On Berkshire's structural advantage of ignoring quarterly earnings.

7. **"It's like shooting fish in a barrel. My idea of shooting fish in a barrel is to drain the barrel first."** (2015 DJCO) — On seeking the easiest possible opportunities.

8. **"A lot of success in life and business comes from knowing what you want to avoid: early death, a bad marriage, etc."** (2000 Wesco)

9. **"You only need one cinch! And sometimes the market gives you the equivalent of an Uncle Horace. And when it does, step up to the pie-cart with a big pan."** (2017 DJCO)

## 6. Story-Telling Patterns

### Pattern A: The Historical Parable

Munger uses historical events as morality tales. His favorites are World War II, the Marshall Plan, the Weimar inflation, the rise of Hitler, and ancient Greece/Rome.

**Example 1: The Punic Wars Tax Lesson**
- **Source**: 2011 Munger's Thoughts
- **Story**: "During the Punic Wars, two-thirds of war debt was repaid before the war was even over. People who paid taxes thought it was the right thing to do. They had a sense of duty and patriotism. I don't like the idea that we can't add new taxes today when faced with such problems."

**Example 2: Germany's Recovery Arc**
- **Source**: 2018 DJCO meeting notes
- **Story**: "After World War I they had a hyper-inflation when the currency basically went to zero in value. They really screwed up big time. And what happened? Well what happened was they recovered from it pretty quick. And they did it by creating a new Reichsmark... That new Reichsmark was working pretty well and Germany had pretty well recovered from that catastrophe and then along came the Great Depression. And the combination of the Great Depression and the Weimar inflation really brought in Hitler. ... By 1939 it's the most prosperous country in Europe. It's encouraging. I hope you feel better."
- **Analysis**: This is classic Munger story architecture: (1) state the disaster, (2) describe the surprising recovery, (3) note the subsequent disaster, (4) extract the universal lesson (civilizations can survive extreme shocks). The punchline "I hope you feel better" shows he knows the story is actually terrifying.

**Example 3: The Marshall Plan**
- **Source**: 2010 Wesco meeting notes
- **Story**: During WWII, Japan tortured US soldiers and Germany put people in ovens. After the war, "We gave them money to rebuild. We said, 'Let bygones be bygones.' The result was a magnificent global economic system and a win for human rights."
- **Analysis**: Munger contrasts WWI revenge (leading to Hitler) with WWII reconstruction (leading to prosperity). The story demonstrates enlightened self-interest through generosity.

### Pattern B: The Personal Failure / Embarrassment Story

Munger tells stories about his own mistakes to build credibility and disarm audiences.

**Example 1: The Blonde Goddess and the Burning Dress**
- **Source**: 2017 DJCO meeting notes
- **Full story**: Munger, as a skinny, underdeveloped freshman, takes a "blonde goddess" to a dance. He pretends to smoke to impress her. She's wearing a net dress. He sets her on fire. He throws Coca-Cola on her to put out the fire. "And that's the last I saw of the blonde goddess."
- **Analysis**: The self-deprecation is extreme. This is a 93-year-old billionaire telling a roomful of admirers about his romantic disasters. The story humanizes him and makes his subsequent success more remarkable.

**Example 2: The Mud-Stuck Ford and the Broken Engine Block**
- **Source**: 2017 DJCO meeting notes
- **Continuation**: His car gets stuck in mud with a girl, they walk miles through sleet, he forgets to put antifreeze in the car, the block cracks, his father refuses to buy him a new one. "My life is just one long litany of mistakes and failure."
- **Analysis**: Munger's self-deprecation reaches its zenith here. The moral he draws — "just keep trying" — has weight precisely because he's established he started from a place of repeated failure.

**Example 3: Losing 50% Three Times**
- **Source**: 2017 DJCO meeting notes
- **Story**: "What happened is the value of my partnership ... went down by 50% in one year. ... That has happened to me 3 times in my Berkshire stock. So I regard it as part of manhood. ... conduct your life so that you can handle the 50% decline with aplomb and grace. Don't try to avoid it. ... In fact I would say if it doesn't come, you're not being aggressive enough."
- **Analysis**: This is Munger's antidote to risk-aversion. Rather than teaching people to avoid losses, he teaches them to build the psychological fortitude to endure them.

### Pattern C: The Anecdote-As-Argument

Munger frequently deploys short, punchy anecdotes that function as encapsulated arguments.

**Example 1: The Dean Kendall Candy Story**
- **Source**: 2007 Wesco meeting notes
- **Story**: "When I was a little boy, I was put in charge of a little retail operation that included candy. My father saw me take a piece of candy and eat it. I said, 'Don't worry. I intend to replace it.' My father said, 'That sort of thinking will ruin your mind. It will be much better for you if you take all you want and call yourself a thief every time you do it.'"
- **Analysis**: Munger uses this to illustrate self-serving bias and rationalization — the danger of believing your own justifications. The father's advice is to label bad behavior honestly, not to paper over it.

**Example 2: The B.B. Robinson Story**
- **Source**: 2018 DJCO meeting notes
- **Story**: A playboy who got rich from 1920s stock pools moves to LA, drinks heavily, chases starlets. His banker calls him in, worried. B.B. Robinson replies: "Listen. My Municipal Bonds don't drink."
- **Analysis**: Munger uses this to explain why DJCO's marketable securities are safe regardless of management quality. The punchline does all the argumentative work.

**Example 3: The Open Heart Surgery Doctors**
- **Source**: 2015 DJCO meeting notes
- **Story**: In Redding, CA, doctors convinced themselves everyone needed open heart surgery. "Nobody survives open heart surgery better than the guy who doesn't need it at all." They made money, achieved status, and other hospitals sent executives to learn from them — for years, before anyone caught on.
- **Analysis**: Used to illustrate the extreme forms of incentive-caused bias. Even life-and-death decisions are corrupted by financial incentives.

**Example 4: Abraham Lincoln and the Widow**
- **Source**: 2019 DJCO meeting notes
- **Story**: A surviving partner wants Lincoln to collect a debt from a poor widow with three children. Lincoln: "You look like an enterprising fellow who could get that much money back pretty easily through a little effort. And if you want to ring a little money out of this poor widow and her three children then you'll have to get a different kind of lawyer."
- **Analysis**: Munger contrasts this with modern politicians. The story demonstrates character through action rather than rhetoric.

**Example 5: The Man Who Lost Everything Selling Puts**
- **Source**: 2005 Wesco, 2009 Wesco (retold)
- **Story**: "I know a man who owned his house free and clear, had $5 million of securities and lived on the income from those securities. But this left him a little short of what he wanted, so he said to himself, 'I'll go do a little work to get my return up,' and he sold puts, backed by his entire account. ... in due course he lost all of his money and now works in a restaurant."
- **Analysis**: The perfect cautionary tale about the danger of reaching for yield. Munger retells it multiple years — it's his go-to warning against greed when you already have enough.

### Pattern D: The Family Story as Value Transmission

Munger uses family stories to transmit values and establish authenticity.

**Example 1: Grandpa Ingham's "Few Opportunities"**
- **Source**: 2017 DJCO, 2019 DJCO
- **Story**: His great-grandfather was a pioneer who came to Iowa with nothing, fought in the Blackhawk Wars, and became the richest man in town. His lesson: "There's just a few opportunities you get in a whole life. ... He bought a few farms every time there was a panic. And leased them to thrifty Germans, you couldn't lose money."
- **Analysis**: This is the origin story of Munger's concentrated investing philosophy. He traces it to family wisdom, not academic theory.

**Example 2: Grandpa Munger the Federal Judge**
- **Source**: 2017 Michigan talk
- **Story**: His grandfather rose from poverty, educated himself, became the longest-serving federal judge. His philosophy: "You have a moral duty to make yourself as un-ignorant and un-stupid as you possibly can."
- **Analysis**: Munger traces his concept of "rationality as a moral duty" directly to his grandfather. The story gives his most abstract principle a concrete, personal origin.

**Example 3: Father's Lesson - Grant McFadden vs. the Blowhard**
- **Source**: 2007 Wesco; 2019 DJCO
- **Story**: His father spent all his time working for a difficult blowhard client rather than his best friend Grant McFadden. Why? The blowhard generated endless legal work; Grant McFadden was so ethical and competent he rarely needed a lawyer.
- **Analysis**: Munger's life philosophy in one story — be a Grant McFadden. The best defense against legal/financial trouble is to behave so well you never generate it.

**Example 4: The Grandpa Who Saved Two of Three Children in the Depression**
- **Source**: 2017 Michigan talk
- **Story**: When the banks closed in 1933, his paternal grandfather took one-third of his assets (good mortgages on homes of "teetotaling German butchers") and injected them into his son-in-law's failing bank, taking the bad assets out — saving two of his three children from financial ruin.
- **Analysis**: Used to illustrate both prudence (the grandfather had conservatively invested in safe mortgages) and family obligation (he sacrificed his own wealth to help his children).

## 7. Interaction with Audience

### Tone and Demeanor

**Humor as the Primary Mode**
- Munger opens the 2000 Wesco meeting: "This only masquerades as a shareholder's meeting. It's really a gathering to hear the thoughts of the assistant headmaster of a cult."
- 2007 Wesco: "I didn't set out in life to become the assistant leader of a cult. As they say, experience is what happens when you're looking for something else."
- 2011 Wesco (last meeting): "You need a new cult hero. I'm doing you all a favor by not having another one of these meetings."
- **Analysis**: Munger is acutely self-aware about the cult-like following. He leans into it with ironic distance — calling his audience "cultists" and "addicts" while simultaneously appreciating their dedication. The "cult" framing appears across 11+ years of meetings.

**The Wise-Ass Streak**
- 2004 Wesco: "I think these things [well-attended meetings] happen when 3-4 things work together. I don't think it would happen if Warren and I didn't have a significant wise-ass streak."
- **Evidence everywhere**: When a questioner calls him "Mr. Buffett" (2018 DJCO), he replies: "I'm flattered to be called Mr. Buffett." When asked whether he considered investing in Tiffany's: "I can buy the candy, but I can't buy the company." When a young investor mentions his 40-year runway: "How are your legs?"

**Abrasive Honesty Disguised as Humor**
Munger uses humor to deliver genuinely harsh messages:
- "I'm not trying to make your morning. I'm just trying to describe things the way they are." (2019 DJCO)
- "If you want to hit it out of the park easily, you should talk to Jim Cramer." (2019 DJCO)
- To an audience member who doesn't read the Wall Street Journal: "What planet do you live on?" (2014 DJCO)
- "You can see the salt tears running down my cheeks" when a competitor has problems with a customer (2017 DJCO)

**The "Socratic Solitaire" Opening**
- Munger frequently begins meetings by asking and answering his own questions — which he calls "Socratic solitaire." This is most developed in the 2009 Wesco meeting, where he asks and answers 8 questions before taking audience questions.
- **Analysis**: This technique allows Munger to control the narrative, set the agenda, and address issues he knows the audience cares about — while modeling how to think rather than what to think.

### Call-and-Response Patterns

Munger frequently polls the audience or draws them into participation:
- 1999: Polls audience on how many stocks they own (90% fewer than 12)
- 2002: Asks who was taught mental models in university (1 person raises hand — from MIT)
- 2015: "How many in this room would have predicted negative interest rates in Europe? Raise your hands." (No hands)
- 2016: "How many people find it hard to make those investments right now?" (Most hands raised) "Yeah, yeah, it's an intelligent group of people. We collect them."

### Contrasts with Buffett

- Munger readily admits Warren is the more optimistic one: "I'm not as optimistic as Warren. I don't see how anybody could be more optimistic than Warren."
- He uses Buffett as a foil for his own darker humor: "The only regret I have it that I'll soon be dead. I deal with it through humor. Some people deal with it through religion; I deal with it through humor."
- Buffett is characterized as the endlessly patient optimist; Munger as the sharp-tongued realist. Their on-stage dynamic — Buffett the expansive explainer, Munger the terse punctuator — is itself a performance that audiences come to see.

### Patience and Impatience

Munger shows genuine patience with:
- Young people asking career/life advice (though he may warn them it's the wrong question)
- Questions about core principles he's explained many times before
- Shareholders who've traveled long distances

He shows active impatience with:
- Questions about specific stocks to buy/sell
- Macro predictions
- Questions outside his circle of competence (quick shutdown)
- Repetitive questions from people who clearly haven't read his past answers

### Physical and Age-Related Humor
- 2008: When the microphone system fails: "We are in shadow of Caltech and we can't get the sound system right."
- 2007: "I've worn out the patience of my listeners, but I've never worn out a microphone before."
- 2019: "I'm 96, Rick is 90, our CEO is 80. I mean, if you're not the crapper, you don't belong here." (misheard — likely "crapper" means someone who shouldn't be there)
- 2018: "We are waiting for some of our directors who are in the restroom. If you have a group of elderly males, they never get together on time."
- **Analysis**: Munger weaponizes his own age for humor. By openly acknowledging his physical decline, he disarms any criticism and creates a unique comedic persona — the ancient sage who's seen everything and can't be bothered to pretend otherwise.

#### Pattern: Confrontation with Authority ("Nutty as a Fruitcake")

> "I had this discussion with this woman who was head of the accounting standards authority, very high IQ women, very high-grade woman. [But] nutty as a fruitcake. **Analysis**: This reveals how Munger confronts institutional gatekeepers. His approach: (1) make the practical-consequences argument directly, (2) be willing to call an authority figure "nutty as a fruitcake" publicly, (3) note the institutional response ("we don't talk that way") as further evidence of dysfunction. [Genuinely NEW interaction]

#### Pattern: Reframing Through History ("When They Are Talking, They Are Lying")

Munger puts the 2008 financial crisis in historical context by comparing it to the Gilded Age:

> "Somebody in Congress accurately described those two men [Jay Gould and Russell Sage]. He said, 'When they are talking, they are lying and when they are quiet they are stealing.' We live in the aftermath of Russell Sage and Jay Gould. They are gone, and we've since had Thomas Edison and Henry Ford. We've had a lot of wonderful achievement and investment banking had a long phase when it behaved better than it has in the recent buildup to sin and folly, and so we can hopefully believe we will go back."

**Analysis**: Munger's characteristic move - place present scandal in historical context, show that civilization survived worse, and suggest that better behavior can return. The "when they are talking, they are lying" line (from a Congressman) is a quote Munger deploys for maximum effect. [Genuinely NEW historical framing]

**Analysis**: Munger uses the "adult problem" label to distinguish between what most people do (rationalize and tolerate bad behavior) and what ethical people should do (refuse). It's a moral framing that dismisses compromise as merely conventional, not principled. [Genuinely NEW conceptual frame]

#### Pattern: "If I Were Lee Kuan Yew" - The Benevolent Dictator Fantasy

**Analysis**: Munger expresses frustration with democratic constraints on reform while acknowledging those constraints are real. ### PCA.4 THE "SELF-MADE SYSTEM" - Munger's Originality

### MT.1 NEW Q&A PATTERNS NOT IN EXISTING DATABASE

#### MT.1.1 The "Swallow Your Pride" Deflection

**Source**: [MT-2021], [MT-2022]

Late-career Munger developed a specific technique for deflecting questions about complex topics he doesn't understand: He frames ignorance as wisdom.

**Example ([MT-2021]):**
Question about hydrogen energy in transportation:
> "很抱歉，你的问题不在我的能力圈范围之内，我答不出来。" (Sorry, your question is outside my circle of competence, I can't answer.)

**Example ([MT-2022]):**
Question about whether Costco is overvalued:
> "按开市客现在的股价，我不一定会买，但我绝对不会卖。" (At Costco's current price, I might not buy, but I would absolutely never sell.)

**Analysis**: This is different from the classic "I have nothing to add" deflection. Here, Munger gives a partial, nuanced answer that refuses binary framing - he won't say "buy" or "sell," but gives his actual mental state (holding through overvaluation because quality is so high).

---

#### MT.1.2 The "Turning the Question Around" Technique (Early Career Version)

**Source**: [MT-1987], [MT-1990], [MT-1993]

Younger Munger more actively reframes questions than the older Munger. The book shows this pattern was deployed from his very first meetings.

**Example ([MT-1990]):**
Question about whether Wesco can replicate past success:
> "我要是真知道如何复制过去的成功，那可好了。其实，大多数时候，我们什么都不做。我们出手的时候很少。即使是出手的时候，我们也是如履薄冰，对可能承担的风险感到不安。"

**Analysis**: He converts a question about "how to win" into an answer about "how not to lose" - turning the question around (inversion as Q&A technique) rather than answering directly.

---

#### MT.1.3 The "Share the Pain" Moment

**Source**: [MT-2021]

When asked about the difficulty of investing:
> "你觉得难的话，说明你的脑子是清醒的，投资当然难了。和我们这代人相比，现在的年轻人，太难了。现在的年轻人，想要出人头地，想要获得财富，必须比我们那时候付出远远更多的努力。"

**Analysis**: This is Munger at his most empathetic - a rare mode. Instead of the Mozart-like "you can't do it" deflection, he validates the questioner's struggle and places it in generational context. This "share the pain" pattern appears more frequently in his very late years (2021-2022).

---

#### MT.1.4 The "Loyalty Disclosure" Pattern

**Source**: [MT-2021], [MT-2022]

When asked about selling, Munger makes his emotional attachment explicit:

**Example ([MT-2021]):**
> "如果我们喜欢一家公司、喜欢它的管理层，比亚迪就是一个例子，我们会对它非常忠诚，甚至有些过于忠诚。这一点，我们不会变。"

**Example ([MT-2022]):**
> "按开市客现在的股价，我不一定会买，但我绝对不会卖。"

**Analysis**: Munger's open admission of "excessive loyalty" is unusual for a value investor. He essentially acknowledges a behavioral bias and refuses to correct for it - a rare departure from pure rationality.

---

#### MT.1.5 The Self-Deprecating Humor About Age (Evolved Form)

**Source**: [MT-2021], [MT-2022]

Munger weaponizes his age with a specific new technique - openly acknowledging his limitations while continuing to operate:

> "我都这个岁数了，对自己的要求放松了很多。回顾这一生，我很知足。别人做得比我好，我也不在意了。" ([MT-2021])

> "我这么大岁数了，去自己想去的地方投资，这点自由还是可以有的。" ([MT-2022])

**Analysis**: Late-career Munger uses age as both shield and sword - it excuses lower standards while justifying contrarian positions (investing in China while Buffett doesn't).

### MT.3 HOW MUNGER'S Q&A STYLE EVOLVED (35-Year Arc)

**Younger Munger (1987-1995):** More technical, more willing to explain specific business operations (loan policies, insurance reserving, real estate development details). More combative tone with regulators. Passionate about S&L crisis analysis. Still establishing his public voice.

**Middle Munger (1996-2005):** Peak "teaching mode." Organizes answers around mental models. References to books and frameworks increase. Starts using the "hammer" metaphor regularly. The "I have nothing to add" trademark emerges at Berkshire meetings.

**Older Munger (2006-2015):** Peak compression. Answers get shorter, sharper, funnier. More one-liners. Less patient with repetitive questions.

**Late Munger (2016-2022):** Visible softening on some edges. More willing to admit he "doesn't know" without edge. More reflective. The "share the pain" mode emerges. Yet the core framework remains extraordinarily consistent - in 2022, at age 98, he is still making the same arguments about speculation, envy, rationality, and value investing that he made in 1987 at age 63.

### MT.4 NEW SPECIFIC Q&A MOMENTS OF NOTE

**1987 - First meeting with shareholders:**
Munger establishes his persona immediately - humble about the future ("我们根本没有预知未来的能力"), skeptical about acquisitions ("真有我们看好的公司，我们会尽全力追求，但现在没一个我们看好的"), and candid about limitations.

**1990 - The Graham IQ test revelation:**
Munger reveals that Ben Graham once administered a trick quiz at a gathering of 150+ IQ investors. Only one person got more than half the questions right - and that was by guessing. The lesson: "如果比你聪明很多的人要骗你，你再怎么判断和分析，也很难不受骗。" This story does NOT appear in our existing corpus.

**2022 - The "not fooling yourself" about macro:**
> "很难预测好公司未来将如何...要预测过去哪家公司发展得好，这很简单，因为过去发生的事，我们已经知道了。要预测将来会怎样，那可就难了。我真说不好。"
